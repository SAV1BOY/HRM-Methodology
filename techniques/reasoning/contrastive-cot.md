# Contrastive Chain-of-Thought

> **One-line summary:** Augments standard chain-of-thought prompting by providing both correct AND incorrect reasoning examples, teaching the model to distinguish valid reasoning patterns from common error patterns.

## Overview

Contrastive Chain-of-Thought (Contrastive CoT), introduced by Chia et al. (2023), enhances the standard few-shot chain-of-thought paradigm by adding *negative* reasoning examples alongside the traditional positive ones. In standard CoT, the model sees only correct exemplars: a question followed by valid step-by-step reasoning leading to the right answer. Contrastive CoT adds a complementary signal by also showing the model what *wrong* reasoning looks like -- specifically, plausible-sounding but flawed reasoning chains that lead to incorrect answers. This contrastive signal helps the model develop a more robust internal representation of what constitutes valid versus invalid reasoning.

The intuition behind contrastive CoT draws from the well-established principle of contrastive learning in machine learning: models learn representations more effectively when trained with both positive and negative examples. In the prompting context, seeing only correct reasoning leaves the model without explicit guidance about common pitfalls. By contrast, when the model sees both "here is how to reason correctly" and "here is a common mistake to avoid," it develops a sharper decision boundary between valid and invalid reasoning paths. The negative examples act as guardrails, steering the model away from frequent error modes.

Empirically, contrastive CoT demonstrates consistent improvements over standard CoT across arithmetic reasoning benchmarks including GSM8K, AQuA, and MATH. The gains are particularly notable on problems where LLMs commonly make specific, predictable errors -- such as misinterpreting the question, applying operations in the wrong order, or making sign errors. The technique is especially appealing because it requires minimal additional effort beyond standard CoT: the practitioner simply annotates each exemplar with an additional incorrect reasoning chain, which can often be generated by identifying the model's own common failure modes.

## How It Works

1. **Select exemplar problems**: Choose representative problems from the target task, as in standard CoT prompting.
2. **Annotate correct reasoning**: For each exemplar, write the correct chain-of-thought leading to the right answer, just as in standard CoT.
3. **Annotate incorrect reasoning**: For the same exemplar, write a plausible but incorrect chain-of-thought that makes a specific, common error and arrives at a wrong answer. The error should be realistic -- the kind a model might actually make.
4. **Format with contrast**: Present both chains in each exemplar, clearly labeled as correct and incorrect, so the model can see the contrast.
5. **Query with new problem**: Append the test question and let the model generate its reasoning, now informed by both positive and negative patterns.

```
Solve the following problems step by step. For each example, I'll show you

{incorrect_chain_1} The answer is {wrong_answer_1}.
```

## Examples

### Example 1: Arithmetic with Common Order-of-Operations Error

**Prompt:**
```
Solve step by step. I'll show both an incorrect and correct approach.

Q: A store has 45 shirts. They receive 3 boxes with 12 shirts each, then sell
half of all shirts. How many shirts remain?

Incorrect reasoning (common mistake):
The store has 45 shirts. They sell half: 45 / 2 = 22.5, round to 22. Then
they receive 3 * 12 = 36 shirts. Total: 22 + 36 = 58 shirts. The answer is 58.
Why this is wrong: The operations are applied in the wrong order. The store
receives shirts BEFORE selling, not after.

Correct reasoning:
The store starts with 45 shirts. They receive 3 * 12 = 36 more shirts, for
45 + 36 = 81 shirts total. Then they sell half: 81 / 2 = 40.5. Since we
can't sell half a shirt, 81 / 2 = 40 remainder 1, so 40 or 41 are sold.
Assuming integer division: 81 / 2 = 40.5, meaning 40 shirts remain (rounding
down). The answer is 40 (or 41 depending on interpretation).

Q: A baker makes 8 pies on Monday, twice as many on Tuesday, and gives away
a third of all pies on Wednesday. How many pies does the baker have left?

Correct reasoning:
```

**Model Output:**
```
Monday: the baker makes 8 pies.
Tuesday: twice as many as Monday = 2 * 8 = 16 pies.
Total before Wednesday: 8 + 16 = 24 pies.
Wednesday: gives away a third = 24 / 3 = 8 pies given away.
Remaining: 24 - 8 = 16 pies.

Note: I make sure to calculate the total first before applying the "give away
a third" operation, avoiding the error of applying it to only one day's count.

The answer is 16.
```

### Example 2: Logical Reasoning with Negation Error

**Prompt:**
```
Solve step by step. I'll show both an incorrect and correct approach.

Q: In a group of 30 people, 18 speak English and 15 speak French. If everyone
speaks at least one of the two languages, how many speak both?

Incorrect reasoning (common mistake):
18 speak English and 15 speak French. People who speak both = people who
speak English + people who speak French - total people = 18 + 15 - 30 = 3.
Wait, I think it should be the overlap. 18 + 15 = 33 total language
"slots." Since there are only 30 people, the extra 3 must be people
counted twice. The answer is 3.
Why this is wrong: Actually, this reasoning happens to get the right answer
but uses confused justification. Let me show a truly wrong version:
Some might think: since 18/30 speak English and 15/30 speak French, the
probability of both = (18/30) * (15/30) * 30 = 9. The answer is 9.
Why this is wrong: Independence assumption is invalid. We cannot multiply
probabilities here because the events are not independent.

Correct reasoning:
Using the inclusion-exclusion principle: |E union F| = |E| + |F| - |E intersect F|.
Since everyone speaks at least one language: |E union F| = 30.
So: 30 = 18 + 15 - |both|. Therefore |both| = 18 + 15 - 30 = 3.
The answer is 3.

Q: In a class of 40 students, 28 passed math, 25 passed science, and 5
failed both subjects. How many passed both?

Correct reasoning:
```

**Model Output:**
```
Using the inclusion-exclusion principle carefully (avoiding the trap of
multiplying fractions as independent probabilities):

Students who passed at least one subject = 40 - 5 = 35 (since 5 failed both).
|Math union Science| = |Math| + |Science| - |Both|
35 = 28 + 25 - |Both|
35 = 53 - |Both|
|Both| = 53 - 35 = 18.

The answer is 18.
```

## When to Use

- Arithmetic and mathematical reasoning tasks where LLMs make predictable, common errors that can be demonstrated as negative examples.
- Multi-step reasoning problems where order-of-operations, sign errors, or misinterpretation of conditions are frequent failure modes.
- Educational or tutoring applications where showing both correct and incorrect approaches is pedagogically valuable.
- Tasks where you have identified specific, recurring error patterns through error analysis of model outputs.
- Benchmarks where standard CoT leaves a meaningful accuracy gap that error-awareness could close.
- Situations where robustness against common pitfalls matters more than raw speed.

## When to Avoid

- No known or predictable common error patterns exist for the task, making the negative examples arbitrary rather than instructive.
- Simple factual recall or single-step tasks where chain-of-thought reasoning itself is unnecessary.
- The additional negative examples might confuse a smaller or less capable model, leading it to reproduce the errors rather than avoid them.
- Token budget is very tight, as contrastive CoT roughly doubles the prompt length of standard CoT.
- Creative, open-ended, or generative tasks where "incorrect reasoning" is not well-defined.
- The model is already near-perfect on the task with standard CoT, providing no room for contrastive improvement.

## Variants

- **Auto-Contrastive CoT:** Generate negative examples automatically by prompting the model to solve a problem incorrectly in a specific way, then using its output as the negative exemplar. Reduces manual annotation effort.
- **Error-Type-Specific CCoT:** Categorize common errors (arithmetic, order, misread, sign) and include negative examples that specifically target each error type relevant to the test problem.
- **Contrastive + Explanation:** Supplement the negative example with an explicit explanation of *why* the reasoning is wrong, making the error pattern more salient to the model.
- **Lightweight Contrastive:** Instead of full negative chains, provide only a brief "Common mistake: [one-sentence error pattern]" note alongside standard CoT exemplars.
- **Self-Contrastive CoT:** Let the model generate both correct and incorrect chains for each exemplar, then select the best correct/worst incorrect pair for maximum contrastive signal.

## Composability

- **CCoT + Self-Consistency:** Sample multiple contrastive-informed reasoning chains and take the majority vote. The contrastive signal reduces the frequency of common errors across samples.
- **CCoT + Chain-of-Thought:** Contrastive CoT is itself a direct enhancement of standard CoT. It can be combined with any CoT variant (few-shot, auto-CoT, complex CoT).
- **CCoT + Few-Shot Selection:** Use automated exemplar selection methods (e.g., k-nearest neighbors) to choose the most relevant contrastive exemplars for each test problem.
- **CCoT + Verification:** After contrastive-CoT-guided generation, apply a separate verification prompt that checks specifically for the error patterns shown in the negative examples.
- **CCoT + Zero-Shot CoT:** Provide negative examples as a "common mistakes to avoid" preamble before the zero-shot CoT trigger phrase.

## Limitations

- Requires identifying and annotating common error patterns, which demands error analysis and domain expertise.
- The additional prompt length from negative examples roughly doubles token cost relative to standard CoT, which may be prohibitive for cost-sensitive applications.
- Poorly chosen negative examples can be counterproductive: if the model latches onto the incorrect pattern rather than learning to avoid it, accuracy may decrease.
- The technique assumes that LLM errors are systematic and predictable; random or rare errors are not well-addressed by contrastive exemplars.
- Effectiveness depends on the quality of the negative examples -- they must be plausible enough to represent real failure modes but clearly labeled as incorrect.
- Smaller models (under 70B parameters) may struggle to correctly process the contrastive signal and could be confused by the presence of incorrect reasoning in the prompt.
- Limited empirical evaluation across diverse task domains; most results focus on mathematical reasoning benchmarks.
